Property Disclosures — What Buyers & Sellers Must Know in New York

New York law requires several property-specific disclosures in real estate transactions. Understanding these upfront prevents problems at closing and protects both buyers and sellers.

The disclosures on this page apply to residential real estate transactions in New York State — particularly in Queens, Long Island, and the NYC metro area. Sellers have legal obligations before and at closing. Buyers have rights to information and to conduct due diligence. Understanding these requirements protects both sides. Questions? 1

Property Condition Disclosure Statement (PCDS)

NY Real Property Law §462 · Required for residential 1–4 unit sales

What it is: Sellers of residential property (1–4 units) are required by New York State law to complete and deliver a Property Condition Disclosure Statement (PCDS) to the buyer before the buyer signs the contract of sale. The PCDS is a standardized form containing approximately 48 questions about the property's condition and history.

What the PCDS covers:

  • Structural components: foundation, roof, walls, floors, basement
  • Mechanical systems: heating, plumbing, electrical, HVAC
  • Environmental hazards: lead paint, radon, asbestos, underground oil tanks, mold
  • Water and sewer: well, municipal water, septic system, public sewer
  • Title and legal: easements, encroachments, violations, open permits
  • Flood zone status and flooding history
  • Known defects, damages, or repairs in the past 10 years
  • Climate-related hazards (added in 2023 update): flooding, storm damage, sea level rise risk

Seller's Obligation

  • Complete the PCDS truthfully based on actual knowledge
  • Deliver to buyer before buyer signs the contract
  • Alternative: provide buyer a $500 credit at closing
  • Discuss with your attorney before choosing the credit option

Buyer's Rights

  • Receive the PCDS before signing the contract
  • Review and ask questions about any items disclosed
  • Use the PCDS as a starting point for the home inspection
  • Not waive the right to inspect based solely on the PCDS

Important: The PCDS is based on the seller's knowledge — it is not a professional inspection and is not a substitute for hiring a licensed home inspector. Buyers should always conduct an independent inspection regardless of what the PCDS says.

2

Lead Paint Disclosure

42 U.S.C. §4852d (Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act) · Homes built before 1978

Who this applies to: Federal law requires lead paint disclosure for the sale of any residential dwelling built before 1978. Lead-based paint was banned for residential use in 1978, but millions of older homes still contain it — including a very high percentage of the Queens housing stock.

Seller Must:

  • Disclose any known lead paint hazards or lead paint presence
  • Provide the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family from Lead in Your Home"
  • Allow buyers at least 10 days to conduct a lead paint inspection (unless waived in writing)
  • Sign the Lead Paint Disclosure Addendum at contract signing

Buyer Should:

  • Review the seller's lead paint disclosure carefully
  • Request a certified lead paint inspection on pre-1978 homes
  • Consider a lead paint risk assessment in addition to visual inspection
  • Be aware of the risks — especially for families with young children

Queens Context: A large portion of Queens residential housing was built before 1978 — including most of Ozone Park, South Ozone Park, Jamaica, Richmond Hill, Woodhaven, and neighboring communities. Lead paint disclosure applies to a significant majority of homes in this area. Buyers with young children should take this disclosure especially seriously and budget for a lead paint inspection.

Health note: Lead paint is a serious health hazard, particularly for children under 6. Exposure can cause developmental delays, learning disabilities, and other serious health effects. Lead paint that is in good condition is generally not an immediate hazard — but deteriorating, chipping, or peeling lead paint requires immediate attention. For more information, visit epa.gov/lead.

3

Smoke Detector & Carbon Monoxide Detector Compliance

NY Executive Law §378 · NYC Administrative Code · Required at closing

What sellers must do: At closing, sellers of residential property in New York must certify that the home has working smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors installed in compliance with state and local law. A signed Smoke Detector and CO Detector Statement of Compliance is typically required at closing.

Requirements under NY and NYC law:

  • Smoke Detectors: Required on every level of the home, including the basement. In NYC, must be installed in each bedroom and outside each sleeping area.
  • Carbon Monoxide Detectors: Required within 15 feet of the primary sleeping area in any home with a gas appliance, oil or gas furnace, or attached garage. NYC requires CO detectors in all dwelling units.
  • Interconnection: In newly constructed homes and significant renovations, smoke detectors must be interconnected so that when one sounds, all sound.
  • 10-Year Sealed Battery Detectors: NYC now requires "10-year sealed battery" smoke and CO combination detectors in certain applications.

Sellers should verify compliance before listing. Replacing outdated detectors before listing is inexpensive and removes a potential closing obstacle. Buyers should test all detectors during the inspection.

Flood Zone Disclosure

FEMA National Flood Insurance Program · NY PCDS §462 (flood zone question included)

PCDS disclosure: The Property Condition Disclosure Statement includes a question about whether the property is located in a FEMA-designated Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA). Sellers must answer truthfully based on their knowledge.

Independent verification recommended: Flood zone designations can change, and sellers may not always have current information. Buyers should independently verify FEMA flood zone status by consulting FEMA's Flood Map Service Center at msc.fema.gov.

Queens-Specific: Several Queens neighborhoods are in or near FEMA Special Flood Hazard Areas, including Howard Beach, Broad Channel, South Ozone Park (areas near the Belt Parkway), and parts of Jamaica and Springfield Gardens. Properties in these areas require flood insurance if financed with a federally backed mortgage. Buyers should factor flood insurance costs into their total housing budget.

As of 2023, NY's updated PCDS also asks about climate-related risks including historical flooding, storm surge damage, and proximity to water bodies — beyond just the FEMA flood zone designation. Buyers should review all climate-related questions carefully.

Radon Disclosure

EPA Radon Guidelines · NY does not mandate radon testing for resale

What is radon: Radon is a naturally occurring radioactive gas that forms from the decay of uranium in soil and rock. It is colorless, odorless, and tasteless — completely undetectable without testing. Radon can seep into homes through cracks in foundations, basement floors, and walls.

New York requirements: New York State does not require radon testing for the sale of residential property. However, sellers must disclose known radon issues on the PCDS, and buyers may request radon testing as a condition of their offer.

Health risk: Radon is the second leading cause of lung cancer in the United States, responsible for approximately 21,000 deaths annually. The EPA recommends testing all homes, especially those with basements. Buyers purchasing any home with a basement should include radon testing in their due diligence.

Radon testing is inexpensive and quick. Short-term tests (2–7 days) are available at hardware stores for under $30. Professional radon testing and mitigation systems are effective — a properly installed mitigation system can reduce radon levels by up to 99%.

6

Oil Tank Disclosure

NY Environmental Conservation Law · PCDS §462 (environmental hazards)

What sellers must disclose: Sellers are required to disclose known underground storage tanks (USTs) and above-ground storage tanks (ASTs) on the PCDS. This includes both active heating oil tanks and any abandoned tanks that have not been properly decommissioned.

Why this matters: Abandoned underground oil tanks are a significant source of environmental liability in Queens and Long Island. Even tanks that have not leaked can create problems — mortgage lenders and title companies typically require evidence that no underground tanks are present, or that existing tanks have been properly removed and documented.

Buyer due diligence: Buyers of older Queens homes should request an oil tank sweep as part of their inspection. A ground-penetrating radar survey can identify the presence of underground tanks before closing. If a tank is found, further testing and documentation will be needed before most lenders will issue a mortgage commitment.

Sellers who are aware of abandoned underground tanks should consult with a licensed environmental contractor and their attorney before listing.

7

Certificate of Occupancy (CO)

NYC Building Code · NY Town Law · Required for legal occupancy

What is a Certificate of Occupancy: A Certificate of Occupancy (CO) is a document issued by the local building department certifying that a structure complies with the applicable building codes and is legally safe and suitable for occupancy. A CO is required for the original structure and for any additions, conversions, or significant renovations.

Seller's obligation: Sellers must be able to provide a Certificate of Occupancy for their home and any additions. In Queens, this means: the original home, any dormer additions, garage conversions, finished basements, sunrooms, and any extension to the living space must have a valid CO or permit on file.

Unpermitted work is a serious issue. Finished basements, garage conversions, and room additions constructed without permits are extremely common in Queens — and can create major problems at closing. Lenders may refuse to provide a mortgage if the appraisal reveals square footage that is not covered by a valid CO. Buyers should specifically ask about permits for all improvements.

How to check: Certificate of Occupancy records and open permits for NYC properties can be searched at nyc.gov/dobproperty (NYC Buildings Department Property Profile Overview). For Nassau and Suffolk County properties, contact the local building department directly.

Sellers with open violations or missing COs should consult with a licensed contractor and attorney before listing. Resolving permit issues pre-listing avoids last-minute closing complications.

MLS / Listing Data Disclaimer

OneKey MLS Rules · Standard Real Estate Data Disclaimer

All listing data, property information, and market data published by Gadura Real Estate LLC — including MLS listings, website listings, and marketing materials — is deemed reliable but is not guaranteed for accuracy or completeness. Buyers and sellers should independently verify all material information before making any real estate decision.

The following information in particular should be independently verified:

  • Square footage: MLS square footage is often approximate and may not reflect actual livable space. It may or may not include finished basements, attic space, or additions. Always verify with an independent appraiser or by measuring.
  • School district and zone: School zone boundaries can change. Verify directly with the applicable school district or the NYC DOE School Finder.
  • Property taxes: Annual property tax amounts can change with assessments and exemptions. Verify with the local assessor's office. Ask specifically about STAR, senior, and other exemptions that the current owner may have that would not transfer.
  • Lot dimensions and acreage: Verify with a licensed surveyor.
  • HOA fees and rules: If applicable, verify current fees, special assessments, and rules directly with the HOA.
  • Zoning classification: Verify current zoning and permitted uses directly with the local building/zoning department.
