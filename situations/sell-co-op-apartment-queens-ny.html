How I Sell a Queens Co-op

1

Review Proprietary Lease & Building Rules

Before listing, I'll review your proprietary lease for flip tax, subleasing restrictions, and any first right of refusal by the co-op. These affect pricing, marketing, and timeline planning.

2

Price & Market the Co-op

I'll price based on recent comparable co-op sales within your building and similar Queens buildings. Co-op pricing is highly specific to the building's financials, flip tax structure, and maintenance levels.

3

Buyer Screening & Board Package

Once we have an accepted offer, I'll guide the buyer through the board package process. A well-prepared package reduces back-and-forth with the board and speeds approval significantly.

4

Board Approval & Closing

After board approval, closing is coordinated with the managing agent, the buyer's attorney, and the title company. At closing, shares and proprietary lease transfer to the buyer, and proceeds (minus flip tax and closing costs) go to you.

Common Questions

FAQ — Selling a Queens Co-op Apartment

What is the co-op board approval process when selling in Queens?

Your buyer must submit a board package (financial statements, tax returns, reference letters, employment verification) for board review. The board may conduct an interview. Approval typically takes 30–90 days in Queens co-ops. Boards can reject buyers for financial reasons but may not discriminate based on protected classes under Fair Housing law.

What is a flip tax and how does it affect my co-op sale?

A flip tax is a fee paid to the co-op corporation when you sell your shares — typically 1–3% of the sale price or a fixed amount per share. It's set by your proprietary lease and is usually paid by the seller at closing. Check your proprietary lease before listing — it effectively reduces your net proceeds and should factor into your pricing strategy.

Do I sell the apartment or the shares in a Queens co-op?

You own shares in the corporation, not the physical apartment. What you're selling is your stock certificate and proprietary lease. The closing involves a stock transfer and assignment of the proprietary lease rather than a real property deed transfer. NY transfer taxes still apply based on the sale price.

How long does it take to sell a co-op apartment in Queens?

Finding a buyer and signing a contract typically takes 30–60 days. Then add 30–90 days for board package preparation and approval. Total timeline from listing to closing: typically 3–5 months. Financially well-qualified buyers experienced with co-op purchases tend to move through approval faster.

Can a co-op board reject my buyer for any reason?

Boards have broad discretion but may not discriminate based on race, color, national origin, sex, disability, familial status, religion, or (in NYC) source of income, sexual orientation, or other protected classes under NY and NYC fair housing laws. Financial qualification is the most common reason for rejection. I vet buyers carefully before submission to reduce this risk.
