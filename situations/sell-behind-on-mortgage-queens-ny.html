Common Questions

FAQ — Selling a Queens Home While Behind on Mortgage

What's the difference between a short sale and a regular sale when behind on mortgage?

A regular sale means you sell at market value and proceeds pay off your mortgage in full at closing — simple and fast. A short sale is when you owe more than the home is worth and your lender agrees to accept less than the balance. Regular sales are faster, simpler, and have no negative credit impact from the sale itself. Many Queens homeowners who think they need a short sale actually have enough equity for a regular sale given recent appreciation.

Can I sell my Queens home if I'm 2–3 months behind on payments?

Yes. Being 2–3 months behind means foreclosure has not yet started. Once you list and get an offer, the outstanding balance plus all arrears (missed payments, late fees) are paid from sale proceeds at closing. You don't need to catch up before selling — the title company handles it all at the closing table.

Will selling catch up all my missed payments?

Yes. At closing, your lender provides a payoff statement that includes the full loan balance plus all accrued interest, late fees, and arrears. This full amount is paid from sale proceeds before you receive any equity. If positive equity remains after payoff, you receive the difference as cash at closing.

How does being behind on mortgage affect the selling price?

It doesn't. Your home is worth what the Queens market says it's worth — not less because of your payment history. Buyers don't see your mortgage status. The payoff (including arrears) simply comes out of your proceeds at closing. A well-priced Queens home will still attract strong buyer competition regardless of your payment history.

What if there isn't enough equity to pay off the mortgage and arrears?

If the sale price won't cover the full mortgage payoff plus arrears, a short sale requires lender approval. I'll help you approach your lender's loss mitigation department. Alternatively, if you're close to breakeven, you may be able to bring a small amount of cash to closing to bridge the gap and avoid the short sale process entirely.
