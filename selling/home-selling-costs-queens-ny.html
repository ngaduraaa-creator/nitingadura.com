Selling Guide

Home Selling Costs in Queens, NY — Complete 2024 Guide

Every dollar you'll spend selling a home in Queens — itemized, explained, and illustrated with a real $750,000 sale example. No surprises at the closing table.

Most Queens sellers are focused on the sale price — and rightfully so. But your net proceeds depend on understanding every cost that comes out before you receive your wire. In Queens, total selling costs typically run 6–8% of the sale price, sometimes more for co-ops with flip taxes.

Here's a complete breakdown of every category of selling cost, followed by a full line-item example for a $750,000 Queens home sale.

1. Real Estate Broker Commission

Post the August 2024 NAR settlement, buyer broker compensation is no longer automatically bundled into the listing side. Here's what this means in practice:

  • Listing agent commission: You negotiate this directly with your listing agent. Typical range in Queens: 2–3% of the sale price.
  • Buyer agent commission: Now negotiated separately between buyers and their agents, and disclosed. Many sellers in Queens still offer a cooperative commission to buyer agents (typically 2–2.5%) to maximize buyer pool and showings. This is now optional but strategically important.
  • Total effective commission: In most Queens transactions, sellers still budget 4–5% total, though this is now explicitly negotiated rather than automatically applied.

NAR Settlement Impact (2024): The rules changed in August 2024. Buyer agent compensation is no longer automatically included in the MLS listing. Sellers can choose to offer it, and the amount must be disclosed to buyers. This increased transparency but hasn't dramatically changed average commission totals in Queens — most sellers are still offering buyer agent compensation to attract broad buyer representation.

2. New York State Transfer Tax

Paid by the seller at closing. NYS transfer tax rates for residential property:

  • 0.4% (4 per $1,000) on sales under $3 million
  • 0.65% ($6.50 per $1,000) on the entire amount for sales of $3 million or more

On a $750,000 sale: 0.4% × $750,000 = $3,000

3. NYC Real Property Transfer Tax (RPTT)

In addition to the state tax, New York City charges its own transfer tax paid by the seller:

  • 1% of the sale price for residential sales under $500,000
  • 1.425% for residential sales of $500,000 or more
  • 1.65% for sales over $3,000,000 (additional progressive rate)

On a $750,000 sale: 1.425% × $750,000 = $10,688

NYC Mansion Tax (Buyer Pays): The NYC mansion tax — 1% on sales $1M–$1.999M, escalating to 3.9% over $25M — is paid by the buyer, not the seller. It's not your cost, but it affects buyer affordability. For homes priced just over $1M, buyers often negotiate harder to offset their mansion tax exposure.

4. Real Estate Attorney Fees

New York requires attorneys on both sides of a real estate transaction. Seller attorney fees in Queens typically run:

  • Standard residential transaction: $1,500–$2,500
  • Complex transactions (estates, co-ops, short sales): $2,500–$4,000+
  • Some attorneys charge hourly ($250–$400/hr) rather than a flat fee

Budget $2,000 as a reasonable estimate for most Queens sales.

5. Co-op Flip Tax (Co-ops Only)

If you're selling a co-op apartment, your building almost certainly has a flip tax — a fee the co-op corporation charges on the sale, paid by the seller. Common structures:

  • Percentage of gross sale price: Typically 1–3% (most common in Queens)
  • Per-share amount: $5–$15 per proprietary lease share
  • Percentage of profit: Applied to the gain over your original purchase price
  • Flat fee: Less common; a fixed dollar amount regardless of price

Always check your proprietary lease before pricing. A 2% flip tax on a $400,000 co-op sale = $8,000 coming off your net. This is a significant cost many sellers forget to account for.

6. Pre-Sale Repairs and Improvements

The amount spent varies enormously. A well-maintained home may need only $2,000–$5,000 for cosmetic touch-ups. A home that needs updating before listing may require $15,000–$30,000+. Common pre-sale investments in Queens:

  • Fresh interior paint (1–2 rooms or full house): $1,500–$6,000
  • Hardwood floor refinishing: $2,500–$5,000
  • Kitchen/bath cosmetic updates: $3,000–$15,000
  • Roof repair or replacement: $8,000–$20,000 (may be required after inspection)
  • Electrical panel upgrade: $3,000–$6,000

I can help you identify which improvements will yield the best return vs. which are better left to the buyer as a price concession.

7. Staging

  • DIY / consultation only: $300–$600 (stager walks the home, provides a room-by-room action list)
  • Partial staging (key rooms): $1,500–$3,500/month
  • Full vacant staging: $4,000–$8,000 for the first month

For occupied homes in Queens (most common), a consultation plus targeted decluttering is often sufficient. For vacant homes over $800K, professional staging almost always pays for itself in faster days-on-market and stronger offers.

8. Moving Costs

  • Local move (within Queens/NYC): $1,200–$2,500 for a 3-bedroom
  • Long Island or outer NYC suburbs: $2,000–$4,000
  • Out of state: $5,000–$12,000+
  • Storage (if needed between sell and buy): $150–$400/month

9. Miscellaneous Closing Costs

  • Title company fees (seller's portion): Typically $250–$600 (the buyer pays most title costs, but sellers sometimes share transfer/search fees)
  • Deed preparation: Often included in attorney fee, or $150–$300 separately
  • Co-op fees (if applicable): Move-out fees, elevator deposits, managing agent fees — $500–$2,000
  • Home warranty (optional, offered to buyer): $400–$700
  • Payoff of existing mortgage: This is not technically a cost — it comes from your proceeds — but you need to know your payoff balance to calculate net proceeds accurately. Call your lender for a 30-day payoff statement.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the NY State transfer tax on a home sale?

New York State imposes a transfer tax paid by the seller: 0.4% on sales under $3 million, and 0.65% for sales of $3 million or more. On a $750,000 Queens sale, that's $3,000 in NYS transfer tax.

Who pays the NYC mansion tax?

The NYC mansion tax is paid by the buyer, not the seller. It applies to sales of $1 million or more, starting at 1% and escalating for higher prices. You don't pay it, but it can affect how buyers negotiate on homes priced near or above $1 million.

How much is broker commission after the NAR settlement?

After the August 2024 NAR settlement, buyer broker compensation is no longer automatically a seller obligation. Sellers negotiate their listing agent's commission directly. Most Queens sellers still offer buyer agent compensation (typically 2–2.5%) to attract broad representation, but this is now explicitly negotiated and disclosed.

What is a co-op flip tax and who pays it?

A flip tax is a fee charged by a co-op corporation when a unit is sold, almost always paid by the seller. Common structures: 1–3% of the gross sale price, a per-share amount, or a percentage of profit. Always check your proprietary lease before pricing your co-op.

Do I need a real estate attorney to sell my home in Queens?

Yes. New York is an attorney state. You must have a licensed NY real estate attorney to execute the purchase contract and handle closing. Attorney fees for sellers in Queens typically range from $1,500 to $3,000.
