How to Sell a Co-op Apartment in Queens, NY

Board packages, flip taxes, proprietary leases, and what co-op boards actually look for — based on real co-op transactions in Ozone Park, Howard Beach, and Richmond Hill.

Selling a co-op in Queens is fundamentally different from selling a house or condo. You're not selling real property — you're selling shares of a corporation and transferring the right to occupy a unit under a proprietary lease. The co-op board has approval authority over your buyer, which adds a layer of complexity (and timeline) that surprises first-time co-op sellers every time.

Here's what you actually need to know.

What You're Actually Selling in a Co-op Transaction

When you sell a co-op, you're transferring:

  • Shares of stock in the co-op corporation (proportional to your unit size)
  • The proprietary lease — your right to occupy the specific unit

Because there is no deed transfer (the building is owned by the corporation), the transaction is treated differently for legal and tax purposes. There's no NY State deed, no title insurance (typically), and the closing is simpler on some levels — but the board approval process adds significant complexity.

Step-by-Step: The Co-op Sale Process

1

Review Your Proprietary Lease and House Rules

Before anything else, locate and read your proprietary lease. It contains the flip tax formula, subletting policies, pet restrictions, alteration procedures, and any right-of-first-refusal by the co-op. Your attorney should review this document in full. Many sellers are surprised to find flip tax obligations or restrictions they didn't know about.

2

Request Financial Documents from the Managing Agent

Buyers will want to review the building's financial health before committing. Request from your managing agent: the most recent two years of audited financial statements, current year budget, board meeting minutes (typically 1–2 years), current maintenance schedule, any pending assessments, and the building's underlying mortgage information. Having these ready speeds up the buyer's due diligence significantly.

3

Price the Unit with Co-op Comps Only

Co-op values are different from condo values in the same building or neighborhood. Financing options are more restricted (some lenders don't offer co-op loans; many require larger down payments), and the board approval process limits the buyer pool. Your CMA must use only recent co-op sales in similar buildings — not condos or houses — to price accurately.

4

Accept an Offer and Execute the Contract

Once you accept an offer, your attorney drafts the purchase contract. This is the same attorney-negotiated process as any NY real estate transaction. The buyer typically pays 10% of the purchase price into escrow at contract signing. The contract will include a board approval contingency — if the board rejects the buyer, the deal falls apart and the buyer recovers their deposit.

5

Buyer Prepares the Board Package (1–3 Weeks)

The buyer assembles a comprehensive application. A complete board package typically takes 2–3 weeks to prepare. Delays in package preparation extend your closing timeline. I advise my sellers' buyers on what makes a strong package — a buyer with an incomplete or disorganized package risks rejection for reasons that have nothing to do with their qualifications.

6

Board Reviews the Package (2–6 Weeks)

After submission, the managing agent forwards the package to the board. The board reviews all documents, sometimes requests additional information, and schedules an interview. Some boards meet weekly; others meet monthly. This is often the most timeline-critical factor in the sale — boards that meet monthly can add 3–4 weeks to the process if the buyer misses a meeting cycle.

7

Board Interview

Most Queens co-op boards require an in-person interview (some have moved to video). The buyer meets with 2–5 board members for 20–45 minutes. They're evaluating personality fit, financial stability, and intent (primary residence vs. investment). The interview is often the most anxiety-inducing part for buyers — preparation matters. I help buyers understand what to expect so they present well.

8

Board Approval or Rejection (1–2 Weeks After Interview)

The board votes after the interview. Approval is communicated through the managing agent, not directly to the buyer or seller. If approved, the sale proceeds to closing — typically 2–3 weeks after approval. If rejected, the contract is terminated and the buyer's deposit is returned. The board is not required to state a reason for rejection, as long as rejection is not discriminatory.

9

Closing

Co-op closings in Queens take place at the managing agent's office or a law firm, with both attorneys, sometimes a managing agent representative, and occasionally a lender representative present. You sign stock certificates, transfer the proprietary lease, and receive net proceeds after the flip tax, attorney fees, and any other closing charges are deducted. Closings typically take 1–2 hours.

What Co-op Boards in Queens Actually Look For

Based on board packages I've helped prepare and submitted in Queens co-ops in Ozone Park, Howard Beach, Richmond Hill, and surrounding neighborhoods, here are the financial benchmarks boards most commonly apply:

  • Debt-to-income ratio: Most boards want total housing costs (maintenance + mortgage payment) to be 25–30% or less of gross monthly income. Some are stricter at 25%.
  • Post-closing liquidity: Many boards require buyers to retain 12–24 months of combined maintenance and mortgage payments in liquid assets (cash, stocks, bonds — not retirement accounts) after the down payment. In practice, for a $400,000 co-op with a $3,500/month combined payment, that means $42,000–$84,000 in liquid assets post-closing.
  • Credit score: Most boards don't see the score directly, but lenders do. A buyer with a 680+ credit score is generally able to obtain financing; boards often assess creditworthiness through the lender's commitment letter.
  • Stable employment history: At least 2 years at current employer or in current profession is preferred. Self-employed buyers need 2 years of tax returns showing consistent income and may face more scrutiny.
  • Intent to occupy: The vast majority of Queens co-op boards require buyers to use the unit as their primary residence and do not permit subletting (or restrict it to very limited circumstances). Buyers planning to rent out the unit will be rejected.
  • References: Both personal and professional. Letters should be substantive — generic references are less persuasive than specific, personalized ones from credible individuals.

Queens Co-op Markets: Ozone Park, Howard Beach, Richmond Hill

Co-ops in these neighborhoods are predominantly mid-rise buildings from the 1960s–1980s with active community-oriented boards. Boards in these communities tend to value stability, primary occupancy, and financial conservatism. Buyers with strong employment letters, clean financials, and genuine references from community members typically fare best. I've navigated board submissions in all three neighborhoods and understand what each building's culture expects.

Subletting Restrictions and What They Mean for Your Listing

Most Queens co-ops have strict subletting policies — many prohibit subletting entirely, and others allow it only after a minimum ownership period (often 2–3 years) and with annual board approval. This restriction affects your buyer pool significantly:

  • Investors who want to rent out the unit cannot buy in non-subletting buildings
  • Buyers who might relocate temporarily cannot sublet while away
  • Buyers on temporary visas or with uncertain long-term plans are a higher risk for boards

As a seller, you need to communicate your building's subletting policy accurately in all marketing materials. Buyers who discover sublet restrictions after going into contract may attempt to renegotiate or walk away.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to sell a co-op apartment in Queens?

Selling a co-op in Queens typically takes 14–24 weeks total: 2–4 weeks preparation, 2–6 weeks on market, 1–2 weeks contract, and 4–8 weeks for board approval. The board approval timeline is the most variable factor — boards that meet monthly can push approval to 10–12 weeks.

What is a co-op flip tax in Queens?

A flip tax is a fee charged by the co-op corporation when a unit is sold, almost always paid by the seller. Common formulas include 1–3% of the gross sale price, a per-share amount ($5–$15 per share), or a percentage of profit. Always check your proprietary lease for the exact formula before pricing — it significantly affects your net proceeds.

What does a co-op board look for when approving a buyer in Queens?

Queens co-op boards typically evaluate: debt-to-income ratio (25–30% of gross income), post-closing liquidity (12–24 months of maintenance + mortgage in liquid assets), employment stability, credit history, personal and professional references, and intent to use the unit as a primary residence.

What is a co-op board package and what does it include?

A board package is the buyer's comprehensive application to the co-op for approval. It typically includes 2 years of tax returns, 2–3 months of bank and brokerage statements, employment verification, credit authorization, personal references (2–4 letters), professional references (2–4 letters), the purchase application, and sometimes a personal statement.

Can a co-op board reject a buyer without explanation?

Yes. New York co-op boards have broad discretion to reject buyers and are not required to provide reasons for rejection. However, boards cannot reject buyers for discriminatory reasons under the Fair Housing Act and NYC Human Rights Law (race, national origin, religion, sex, disability, familial status, sexual orientation, gender identity, or source of income).
