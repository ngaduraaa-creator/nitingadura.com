Capital Gains Tax When Selling Your Home in NY — Queens Guide

Federal exclusions, NYS rates up to 10.9%, NYC add-on tax, stepped-up basis for inherited homes, and 1031 exchanges — explained with real Queens scenarios.

Tax Disclaimer: This guide is for educational purposes. It is not tax or legal advice. Your capital gains tax situation depends on your individual circumstances — filing status, income, residency, holding period, and other factors. Consult a qualified CPA or tax attorney before making decisions based on tax considerations.

Queens homeowners who bought a decade or more ago are sitting on enormous gains. A home purchased in Ozone Park for $300,000 in 2012 may be worth $750,000 or more today — a $450,000 gain. Understanding how that gain is taxed (and how much you can shelter) is one of the most financially significant questions of the entire sale.

Step 1: Federal Capital Gains — The Primary Residence Exclusion

Under IRC Section 121, if you've owned and lived in your home as your primary residence for at least 2 of the past 5 years, you can exclude from federal capital gains tax:

  • $250,000 of gain if you file as Single
  • $500,000 of gain if you file as Married Filing Jointly

This exclusion applies regardless of your income. It can be used once every 2 years. The 2-of-5-year requirement does not need to be continuous — just a cumulative 730 days of primary residence use within the 5 years before the sale.

Step 3: New York City Income Tax

NYC residents pay an additional local income tax on capital gains. NYC rates range from 3.078% to 3.876% depending on income. Combined NYS + NYC marginal rates for high-income NYC residents can exceed 14% on capital gains — one of the highest effective rates in the United States.

Inherited Property: Stepped-Up Basis

Inheriting a Queens home is one of the most tax-favorable real estate events. Under IRC Section 1014, inherited property receives a stepped-up basis — your cost basis becomes the fair market value of the property on the date of the decedent's death, not what they originally paid.

Example: Your parent bought a home in Richmond Hill for $80,000 in 1985. At death in 2022, the home was worth $580,000. You inherit it and sell it in 2024 for $620,000.

  • Your basis: $580,000 (stepped up to date-of-death value)
  • Sale price: $620,000
  • Taxable gain: $40,000 (not $540,000)

The step-up effectively erases a lifetime of appreciation from taxation. If you sell shortly after inheriting and the value hasn't changed much, your gain — and your tax — can be minimal or zero.

Investment Property: The 1031 Exchange

If you own a rental property, multi-family building, or any investment property in Queens (not your primary residence), you can defer capital gains tax through a 1031 Like-Kind Exchange (IRC Section 1031):

  • Sell the investment property ("relinquished property")
  • Within 45 days, identify a replacement property of equal or greater value
  • Close on the replacement within 180 days of the original sale
  • Use a qualified intermediary — you cannot touch the proceeds directly
  • All gain is deferred (not eliminated) until you eventually sell the replacement property without doing another 1031

1031 exchanges are powerful tools for Queens landlords who want to sell but avoid a large tax event. They do not apply to primary residences.

Reducing Your Taxable Gain: What Counts as Basis

Your taxable gain = Sale Price − Adjusted Basis. Your adjusted basis includes:

  • Original purchase price
  • Closing costs paid at purchase (title insurance, attorney fees, points)
  • Capital improvements made during ownership (new roof, addition, kitchen renovation — not repairs)
  • Minus any depreciation claimed if used as rental property

Keep records of all capital improvements throughout your ownership. They directly reduce your taxable gain.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How much capital gains tax do I pay when selling my home in New York?

If you qualify for the federal primary residence exclusion ($250,000 single / $500,000 married), you may owe little or no federal capital gains tax. However, New York State taxes capital gains as ordinary income at rates up to 10.9%, and NYC residents pay an additional 3.876%. A Queens homeowner with a large gain may owe combined NYS + NYC taxes even after the federal exclusion — making early tax planning critical.

What is the federal capital gains exclusion for selling a home?

Under IRC Section 121, homeowners can exclude up to $250,000 (single) or $500,000 (married filing jointly) of capital gain from a primary residence sale, provided they have owned and lived in the property for at least 2 of the past 5 years. This exclusion can be used once every 2 years.

What is the capital gains tax on a home I inherited in Queens?

Inherited property receives a stepped-up basis to fair market value at date of death. If you inherit a Queens home worth $600,000 and sell it for $600,000 shortly after, your taxable gain is zero. If it appreciates to $650,000 before you sell, your gain is only $50,000 — not the full appreciation from your parent's original purchase price.

Can I avoid capital gains tax with a 1031 exchange?

A 1031 exchange lets you defer capital gains tax by selling one investment property and reinvesting in a like-kind investment property within 45 days (identification) and 180 days (closing). It applies only to investment or business property — not your primary residence. Queens rental properties and multi-family buildings qualify.

Does New York State tax capital gains from home sales separately?

No. New York State taxes capital gains as ordinary income at rates from 4% to 10.9%. NYC residents pay an additional local income tax of up to 3.876%. Combined, high-income NYC residents face marginal rates exceeding 14% on capital gains — among the highest in the nation.
