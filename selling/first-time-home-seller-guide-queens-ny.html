Selling Guide

First-Time Home Seller Guide for Queens, NY

If you've never sold a home before, this guide walks you through every stage — from decluttering and pricing to offers, inspections, and what actually happens on closing day. Straightforward, no jargon.

First: Take a Breath.

Selling your first home feels enormous — and it is a significant financial event. But thousands of Queens homeowners go through this process every year, and with the right team in place, it's very manageable. This guide exists so you're never caught off guard. Read it, ask me questions, and go into this with your eyes open. You'll be fine.

Stage 1: Getting Ready

Step 1

Get a Comparative Market Analysis (CMA)

Before you do anything else — before repairs, before staging, before setting your timeline — get a CMA. A CMA uses recent closed sales near your property to establish a realistic market value range. Zillow's "Zestimate" is not a CMA; it's an algorithm that can be off by 10–20% in any direction. An accurate CMA from a licensed agent with Queens market knowledge is the foundation of a successful sale. I provide these at no charge.

Step 2

Hire a Real Estate Attorney

New York requires attorney representation for both buyer and seller at every closing. Hire your attorney early — ideally before you even list. Your attorney reviews the purchase contract, negotiates key terms, handles the closing, and makes sure you're protected at every stage. Expect $1,500–$3,000 in attorney fees. Ask me for referrals to experienced Queens real estate attorneys if you need them.

Step 3

Declutter and Depersonalize

This step has the best ROI of anything you'll do. Buyers need to see the home — not your stuff. Remove 50–70% of what's on shelves, counters, and walls. Box up family photos, kids' artwork, and personal collections. Pare furniture down to the essentials. Store the rest in a storage unit or a relative's garage. The goal is a home that feels spacious, clean, and easy for a buyer to picture themselves in. One weekend of serious decluttering can add meaningful perceived value.

Step 4

Make Smart Pre-Sale Repairs

Not every repair is worth making before a sale. Focus on high-visibility, low-cost fixes that affect buyer first impressions: fresh paint in neutral colors, clean grout and caulk in bathrooms, repaired holes in walls, tightened fixtures, functioning light switches, and clean flooring. Avoid expensive renovations (new kitchen, bathroom gut) unless your CMA indicates they'll pay back more than they cost — they usually don't.

Pre-Listing Checklist for Queens Sellers

  • Declutter every room — surfaces, closets, garage, basement
  • Deep clean entire home including oven, fridge, windows
  • Touch-up paint on scuffed walls, baseboards, trim
  • Replace any burned-out lightbulbs (every light should work)
  • Fix dripping faucets, running toilets, sticking doors
  • Clean or replace HVAC filters
  • Power wash driveway and exterior (curb appeal matters enormously)
  • Mow lawn, trim hedges, plant seasonal flowers
  • Gather documents: deed, survey, CO, utility bills, tax bills
  • Hire your real estate attorney before signing a listing agreement

Stage 2: Listing Your Home

Step 5

Review and Sign the Listing Agreement

A listing agreement authorizes your agent to represent you. Key terms to understand before signing: listing price, commission rate, listing period (typically 90–180 days), marketing plan, and cancellation terms. In New York, listing agreements must be in writing. Read every clause. The agreement establishes your relationship with your agent and the terms of their compensation — don't sign anything you don't fully understand.

Step 6

Professional Photography and Marketing Launch

Your agent arranges professional photography (this should be included in your agent's service — not an add-on). The MLS listing goes live with photos, description, and your home appears on Zillow, StreetEasy, Realtor.com, and dozens of syndicated platforms simultaneously. The first 7–14 days on market are your most important — this is when your home is "new" and showing activity peaks. Make sure the home is show-ready on Day 1.

Step 7

Showings and Open Houses

Showings happen on short notice — be prepared to vacate within 1–2 hours when a buyer's agent requests access. Leave the home for every showing; do not be present. Leave lights on, turn off TV, and make sure the home is spotless. After each showing, your agent collects feedback. Open houses typically run Saturday/Sunday, 12–4pm. After 2–3 weeks of showings, your agent will discuss what the market is telling you about pricing and interest level.

Stage 3: Offers and Negotiation

Step 8

Understanding an Offer

Your first offer can feel exciting or disappointing — either way, don't react emotionally. Analyze it systematically:

Offer ComponentWhat It Means
Offered PriceThe buyer's proposed purchase price. Starting point for negotiation.
Down Payment / Proof of FundsHow much the buyer is putting down. Higher down payment = lower loan-to-value = lower lender risk = stronger buyer.
Financing Pre-ApprovalA letter from a lender. Look for: strong lender, specific loan amount, dated within 60–90 days, no conditions beyond standard verifications.
Mortgage ContingencyBuyer can exit and recover deposit if they can't get financing within X days (standard: 30–45 days). Shorter is better for you as seller.
Inspection ContingencyBuyer can request repairs or exit after inspection. Standard in most offers. Waiving this is rare but possible for well-maintained homes.
Proposed Closing DateWhen the buyer wants to close. Does this work with your plans? Flexibility here can be a negotiating chip.
Earnest Money (Down Payment at Contract)Typically 10% of purchase price, paid to your attorney in escrow at contract signing. If buyer defaults, this is your remedy.

The highest offer is not always the best offer. A cash offer with a short timeline beats a financed offer at 5% over asking with a weak pre-approval and a buyer who needs to sell their current home first. Your agent should evaluate the full offer — not just the price — and advise you on risk.

Step 9

Counteroffers and Acceptance

Your response options: accept as-is, counter (adjust price, closing date, contingencies, or other terms), or reject. Most Queens transactions involve at least one round of negotiation. Your agent presents your counteroffer verbally to the buyer's agent, then you execute in writing once agreed. Once both parties have verbally agreed, your attorney prepares the written contract.

Stage 4: Under Contract — The Due Diligence Period

Step 10

Home Inspection

The buyer schedules a home inspection within the first week or two after contract signing. You are not required to be present — and it's better if you're not. The inspector spends 2–4 hours examining the property and delivers a report to the buyer, not you. The buyer may then request repairs, a price reduction, or credits. Do not panic over a long inspection report — all homes have items. Your agent will help you distinguish between reasonable requests and overreaching ones, and advise you on how to respond.

Step 11

Appraisal (Financed Sales)

If the buyer is financing, the lender orders an independent appraisal to confirm the home's value supports the loan amount. If the appraisal comes in at or above the purchase price, the sale proceeds normally. If the appraisal comes in below the contract price, you have three options: reduce the price to the appraised value, ask the buyer to make up the gap in cash, or renegotiate. Your agent will provide comparable sales data if there's a legitimate case for rebutting a low appraisal.

Step 12

Attorney Review and Title

Your attorney ensures title to the property is clear — no outstanding liens, judgments, open permits, or title defects that would prevent transfer. If issues arise (an old unpaid contractor lien, an open permit from a prior renovation), your attorney addresses them before closing. Start gathering documents early: your deed, any prior title reports, survey, CO certificates, and mortgage information.

Stage 5: Closing Day

Step 13

Final Walkthrough

24–48 hours before closing, the buyer does a final walkthrough. They're confirming: the home is in the same condition as when they made their offer, agreed repairs have been completed, and all included items (appliances, fixtures) are still in place. The home should be in move-out condition — completely empty of your belongings (unless otherwise agreed), clean, and accessible.

Step 14

Closing Day

Closing takes place at a law office or title company, typically with both attorneys present. You'll sign: the deed transferring ownership, NYS transfer tax forms, NYC RPTT forms, and various closing documents. Your attorney handles all disbursements from the closing proceeds — paying off your mortgage, paying transfer taxes and fees, paying their fee, and wiring or presenting your net proceeds. The process takes 2–4 hours. You hand over keys, and the home is no longer yours. You're done.

The process has many moving parts and some tense moments (inspection negotiations, appraisal uncertainty, closing delays). Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What should I do first when deciding to sell my house in Queens?

Start with a Comparative Market Analysis (CMA). Before doing anything else — before repairs, before staging, before deciding your timeline — you need to know what your home is actually worth in today's Queens market. A CMA from a licensed agent uses recent closed sales near your property to establish a realistic price range. I provide CMAs at no cost and no obligation.

How do I evaluate an offer on my home in Queens?

Look beyond the headline price. A strong offer has a competitive price, substantial pre-approval or proof of funds, minimal or well-structured contingencies, a realistic closing timeline, and a track record of reliability. A cash offer at 97% of asking can be stronger than a financed offer at 102% with weak pre-approval. Your agent should help you evaluate the full picture.

What is a listing agreement and what should I look for?

A listing agreement is a contract authorizing your agent to represent you in the sale. Key terms: listing period (typically 3–6 months), commission rate, marketing plan, asking price, and cancellation terms. In New York, listing agreements must be in writing. Read every line before signing.

Do I need to be home during showings?

No — it's strongly recommended that sellers are NOT present during showings. Buyers feel more comfortable examining and discussing the home openly when the seller isn't there. Your agent attends in your place or coordinates lockbox access. Before any showing, remove valuables, pets, and personal medications from easy view.

What happens at closing when selling a home in Queens?

Closing takes place at a law office or title company with both attorneys present. You sign a deed transferring ownership, state and city tax forms, and final settlement documents. Your attorney handles disbursement — paying off your mortgage, paying taxes and fees, and wiring your net proceeds. The process typically takes 2–4 hours. You hand over the keys and you're done.
