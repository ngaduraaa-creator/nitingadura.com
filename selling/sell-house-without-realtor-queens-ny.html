Selling Guide

Sell Your House Without a Realtor in Queens? Read This First.

An honest, data-based look at FSBO in New York — what you're taking on, what the numbers say, and what most sellers don't figure out until they're already in trouble.

Every year, some Queens sellers decide to go it alone — skip the agent, save the commission, handle it themselves. I respect the instinct to control costs. But FSBO in New York is fundamentally different from FSBO in Texas or Florida, and most sellers who choose it aren't fully aware of what they're walking into.

This guide is honest about both sides. Here's what FSBO actually involves in Queens.

What Most FSBO Sellers Don't Know Going In

New York Requires an Attorney — There's No Workaround

Unlike many states where a title company or escrow officer can close a sale, New York law requires a licensed real estate attorney on both sides of every residential transaction. You cannot close a home sale in Queens without an attorney drafting and executing the purchase contract.

This matters for FSBO sellers because: You still need an attorney. You are not avoiding professional fees entirely — you're just shifting from agent + attorney to attorney only. Budget $1,500–$3,000 for your attorney regardless of FSBO status.

FSBO Sellers Don't Have MLS Access

The Multiple Listing Service is the primary database where licensed agents find properties for their clients. Zillow, StreetEasy, and Realtor.com all pull from the MLS — their listings for non-MLS properties are far less prominent and far less accurate.

Without MLS access, your home:

  • Won't appear in agent searches (the source of 87% of buyers)
  • Won't be shown by buyer's agents to their clients as part of normal search
  • Won't appear in automatic email alerts sent to buyers registered with agents
  • Will have a Zillow "For Sale By Owner" tag, which many buyers interpret as a red flag

Flat-fee MLS services (roughly $300–$800) can get your home listed on the MLS without a full-service agent. This solves the exposure problem but still leaves you managing showings, communications, negotiations, contracts, and buyer interactions on your own.

You Still Pay the Buyer's Agent (Usually)

Post-NAR Settlement (2024): Buyer agent compensation is no longer automatically required from sellers. However, most buyers are represented by agents who expect to be paid. FSBO sellers who refuse to offer buyer agent compensation dramatically reduce their buyer pool. In practice, most Queens FSBO sellers who want broad market exposure still offer 2–2.5% to cooperating buyer agents — paying the same commission they were trying to avoid, just without listing-side representation.

Pricing Without Data Is the Biggest Risk

The #1 mistake FSBO sellers make is mispricing. Without access to recent closed sales data (MLS access, not just Zillow estimates), it's difficult to price accurately. The consequences:

  • Overpriced: Your home sits. Every week it doesn't sell, buyers assume something is wrong with it. Price reductions signal weakness and attract lowballers.
  • Underpriced: You leave money on the table. In Queens, $25,000–$50,000 pricing errors are not uncommon for sellers without professional guidance.

NY-Specific FSBO Legal Requirements

As a seller in New York, you have specific legal obligations that are non-negotiable regardless of representation:

  • Property Condition Disclosure Statement (PCDS): Under NY RPL §462, sellers must provide buyers with a completed PCDS or give a $500 credit at closing if they decline. The PCDS covers structural, environmental, and mechanical conditions of the property. Errors or omissions can expose you to legal liability.
  • Lead paint disclosure: For homes built before 1978 (this includes most Queens housing stock), federal law (42 U.S.C. §4852d) requires a lead paint disclosure and EPA pamphlet. Buyers must have 10 days to test before the contract is binding unless they waive this right.
  • Agency disclosure: Under NY RPL §443, buyers must receive an agency disclosure form at the first substantive contact.
  • Certificate of Occupancy / open permits: If your home has an open building permit or lacks a valid CO for any portion, you are required to disclose this. Undisclosed open permits can kill deals at closing.

Legal liability risk: Non-disclosure of known material defects exposes sellers to litigation well after closing. A buyer who discovers an undisclosed issue — water infiltration, structural problem, illegal conversion — can pursue legal action. An experienced agent helps you navigate what must be disclosed and how. Without this guidance, FSBO sellers are especially vulnerable.

When FSBO Might Actually Make Sense

I'll be honest: there are scenarios where FSBO or reduced-commission approaches are reasonable.

  • Selling to a known buyer: If you're selling to a family member, neighbor, or known party who has already agreed on terms, the transaction is relatively straightforward. You still need attorneys on both sides, but agent involvement adds less value in this scenario.
  • Highly unique off-market sale: Some luxury or commercial transactions happen off-MLS between sophisticated parties. This is rare in typical Queens residential sales.
  • Sellers with strong real estate backgrounds: If you're a licensed agent yourself or have deep transactional experience, FSBO is a more viable choice.

The Honest Math on Saving Commission

Let's say you're selling a $700,000 Queens home. You decide to FSBO to save the listing-side commission (~2.5% = $17,500).

What often happens instead:

  • You still offer buyer agent commission: –$17,500 (2.5%)
  • Flat-fee MLS listing: –$500
  • Photography (if not included): –$400
  • Price undercut due to less competitive marketing: –$15,000–$35,000
  • Concessions in negotiation without professional guidance: –$5,000–$15,000
  • Longer days on market, carrying costs: –$2,000–$5,000

The true savings — if any — are often much smaller than the headline commission figure suggests, and may turn negative on a net proceeds basis.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I sell my house without a realtor in Queens, NY?

Yes, you can sell FSBO in Queens. However, New York requires a real estate attorney to handle the contract and closing — that cost is unavoidable. You also lose MLS access, which limits buyer exposure significantly. FSBO sellers in NY typically net less than agent-assisted sellers according to NAR data.

Do I need an attorney to sell my home FSBO in New York?

Yes. New York law requires a licensed real estate attorney to draft and execute the purchase contract and handle closing. Unlike states that use escrow companies, New York requires attorney involvement on both sides of every residential real estate transaction — there is no workaround.

Can a FSBO seller in Queens list on the MLS?

Not directly — only licensed agents and brokers can list on the MLS. FSBO sellers can use flat-fee MLS listing services (typically $300–$800) to get on the MLS, but still must offer buyer agent compensation and manage all communications, showings, and negotiations independently.

Do I still have to pay the buyer's agent if I sell FSBO?

After the 2024 NAR settlement, buyer agent compensation is no longer automatically required. However, most buyers are represented by agents who expect compensation. FSBO sellers who refuse to offer buyer agent commissions significantly reduce their buyer pool. Many FSBO sellers still offer 2–2.5% to buyer agents to maintain full market exposure.

How much less do FSBO homes sell for compared to agent-listed homes?

According to NAR's 2023 Profile of Home Buyers and Sellers, the median FSBO sale price was $310,000 versus $405,000 for agent-assisted sales — a gap of nearly 26%. Multiple studies consistently show FSBO homes net less than agent-represented homes after accounting for all costs.
