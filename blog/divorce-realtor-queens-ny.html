Why You Need a Divorce-Experienced Realtor

Not every real estate agent understands divorce transactions. A standard home sale has one seller with one set of goals. A divorce sale has two sellers who may have competing interests, two attorneys with different negotiation strategies, and a court that may impose its own requirements on the sale process.

I've handled divorce real estate transactions across Queens — from contested sales in Forest Hills co-ops to cooperative sales of two-family homes in Richmond Hill. The difference between a smooth transaction and a disaster often comes down to whether the agent understands the legal framework and can manage the emotional dynamics professionally.

How New York Equitable Distribution Works

New York is an equitable distribution state under Domestic Relations Law Section 236(B). This means marital property — including the family home — is divided fairly, though not necessarily equally. The court considers a long list of factors when determining how to split assets:

  • Length of the marriage — longer marriages tend toward more equal splits
  • Income and earning capacity of each spouse
  • Age and health of both parties
  • Non-financial contributions — homemaking, child-rearing, supporting the other spouse's career
  • Custodial parent's need for housing — the spouse with primary custody may be awarded the home or a larger share of equity to maintain stability for the children
  • Whether the property was acquired before or during the marriage — pre-marital property may be treated differently, especially if marital funds were used for mortgage payments or improvements

Understanding these factors is critical because they determine not just how the proceeds are split, but also the urgency of the sale, whether one spouse can buy the other out, and what price the property needs to achieve for both parties to move forward.

Court-Ordered Sales vs. Voluntary Sales

In an ideal scenario, both spouses agree to sell, choose a listing agent together, agree on a price, and cooperate through the showing and closing process. This is a voluntary sale, and it produces the best financial outcome for everyone.

When spouses cannot agree, the court can order a sale. Under NY RPAPL Article 9, a partition action allows the court to appoint a referee who will manage the sale. Here's the problem with referee sales:

  • The referee may not have real estate marketing expertise
  • The property is often sold at auction or with minimal marketing
  • Sale prices from referee sales are typically 15 to 30 percent below market value
  • Both spouses lose control over the timeline and terms
  • Additional legal fees for the partition action eat into proceeds

This is why I always encourage both parties to agree on a voluntary sale early in the divorce process. Even if the relationship is hostile, agreeing to sell through a neutral agent is almost always in both parties' financial interest.

Working With Both Attorneys

In every divorce transaction, I establish a clear communication protocol with both attorneys from day one. Here's how it works:

  1. Initial coordination call. I speak with both attorneys to understand any stipulations, court orders, or separation agreement terms that affect the sale.
  2. Written updates to both sides. Every significant development — listing, price changes, offers, inspection results, appraisal values — is communicated to both attorneys simultaneously via email.
  3. CMA and market data available to both parties. I provide the same market analysis to both spouses and their attorneys so there's no information asymmetry.
  4. Closing coordination. I work with both attorneys to ensure the closing documents reflect the agreed-upon or court-ordered distribution of proceeds.

This equal-access approach prevents the most common problem in divorce sales: one spouse feeling that the agent is secretly working for the other side.

Protecting Children's Housing Stability

When children are involved, the stakes go beyond money. Courts in Queens consistently prioritize the stability of children's living arrangements, and this influences how — and when — the home should be sold.

In many cases, the custodial parent is awarded exclusive use and occupancy of the marital home until the youngest child reaches a certain age (often 18) or until a triggering event like remarriage. This is called a deferred sale order, and it requires careful financial planning.

I help families evaluate whether a deferred sale makes financial sense. Sometimes keeping the home costs more in maintenance, taxes, and mortgage payments than selling now and using the proceeds for a rental or a smaller purchase. Other times, the children's school district, proximity to extended family, and social ties make staying in the home the better choice.

There's no one-size-fits-all answer. My job is to present the numbers clearly so the family and their attorneys can make an informed decision.

The Tax Implications of Selling During Divorce

Timing matters enormously when it comes to capital gains taxes. Here's the key distinction:

  • Selling while still legally married (filing jointly): Up to $500,000 in capital gains can be excluded under IRC Section 121
  • Selling after the divorce is finalized (filing individually): Each person can only exclude up to $250,000

For Queens homeowners who purchased their property years ago, the difference between a $500K and a $250K exclusion can mean tens of thousands of dollars in federal and state taxes. I always flag this for my clients and their attorneys early in the process.

Additionally, transfer of property between spouses incident to divorce is not a taxable event under IRC Section 1041. If one spouse is buying the other out rather than selling, the buyout itself doesn't trigger capital gains — but the purchasing spouse inherits the original cost basis, which matters when they eventually sell.

FAQ — Divorce Real Estate in Queens

Do both spouses need to agree on a realtor during a Queens divorce?

Ideally, yes. When both spouses agree on one neutral agent, it simplifies communication and lowers costs. If they cannot agree, the court can appoint a referee. I frequently serve as the mutually agreed-upon agent because both parties and their attorneys recognize that a neutral, experienced agent protects everyone's financial interests.

How is home equity split in a Queens divorce?

New York follows equitable distribution — marital property is divided fairly but not necessarily 50/50. The court considers marriage length, income, earning capacity, contributions to the property, and children's needs. Home equity is the sale price minus the outstanding mortgage, closing costs, and commissions.

Can a judge force the sale of a home in a Queens divorce?

Yes. Under NY RPAPL Article 9, the court can order a partition sale and appoint a referee to manage it. Referee sales typically produce lower prices than properly marketed MLS listings. Working with a neutral agent before court intervention almost always yields a better financial outcome.

Should we sell the house before or after the divorce is finalized?

Selling before finalization offers strategic advantages, including the $500,000 joint capital gains exclusion versus $250,000 individually. It also allows both spouses to negotiate terms while they still have leverage rather than relying on a court-determined outcome.

How does Nitin handle communication between divorcing spouses?

I maintain strict neutrality with transparent communication. All significant updates are sent to both spouses and both attorneys simultaneously in writing. I never share one party's private information with the other, and showings are coordinated with both parties' schedules.
