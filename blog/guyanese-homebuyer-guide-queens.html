Why I Wrote This Guide for the Guyanese Community

Richmond Hill is often called Little Guyana, and for good reason. Liberty Avenue between Lefferts Boulevard and 133rd Street is the cultural and commercial heart of the Guyanese diaspora in New York. The roti shops, the doubles vendors, the gold jewelry stores, the rum shops — this is home for tens of thousands of Guyanese families.

But when it comes to buying a home, many Guyanese families in Queens face challenges that generic real estate advice doesn't address. Multi-generational living is not optional — it's how families operate. Rental income from a legal apartment isn't a bonus — it's how the mortgage gets paid. And the transition from renting to owning often involves financial structures that mainstream lenders don't immediately understand.

I work in this community every day. My office is in Ozone Park, minutes from Liberty Avenue. I understand how Guyanese families buy homes because I've sat at those kitchen tables, looked at those bank statements, and walked through those two-family houses on 120th Street and 130th Street more times than I can count.

Best Neighborhoods for Guyanese Homebuyers

Here are the Queens neighborhoods where I most frequently help Guyanese families find homes, along with what makes each area special:

Richmond Hill

The center of Guyanese life in New York. Liberty Avenue is the main commercial strip with Caribbean restaurants, grocery stores, remittance offices, and cultural organizations. Housing stock is predominantly two-family and three-family homes with separate entrances — ideal for multi-generational families. Prices range from $650,000 to $1.2 million for a two-family home depending on condition and lot size.

South Ozone Park

Bordering Richmond Hill, South Ozone Park offers similar housing stock at slightly lower price points. Strong Guyanese and Caribbean presence. Excellent access to the A train and JFK Airport. Many families who work at JFK prefer this area for the short commute. Two-family homes range from $600,000 to $950,000.

Ozone Park

A diverse neighborhood with growing Guyanese, Indian, and Caribbean communities. More affordable entry points than Richmond Hill with good transit access via the A train. Housing includes single-family detached homes, attached row houses, and two-family properties. Prices range from $550,000 to $900,000 for two-family homes.

Howard Beach

A quieter, more suburban feel with waterfront areas and larger lot sizes. Growing Guyanese community, especially along the northern sections closest to Ozone Park. Single-family homes with yards and garages. Prices range from $600,000 to $1.1 million. Popular with Guyanese families looking for more space and a suburban lifestyle while staying close to the community.

Jamaica

Strong Caribbean community with excellent transit access — the E, J, Z trains and LIRR all converge in Jamaica. The downtown area is undergoing significant development. Affordable two-family and three-family homes from $500,000 to $850,000, making it an attractive entry point for first-time Guyanese buyers who are priced out of Richmond Hill.

Multi-Generational Homes — What to Look For

In the Guyanese community, buying a home is rarely an individual decision. Parents, grandparents, adult children, and sometimes aunts and uncles all need to be accommodated. Here's what I look for when searching for multi-generational properties:

  • Legal two-family status: The property must have a certificate of occupancy (C of O) or certificate of completion that authorizes two separate dwelling units. Without this, the rental income cannot be counted by most lenders, and you face potential fines from the NYC Department of Buildings.
  • Separate entrances: Ideally, each unit has its own street-level entrance so family members have privacy and independence.
  • Separate utility meters: Gas and electric should be metered separately. This is important for fairly splitting costs and for tax documentation if you rent to non-family.
  • Kitchen in each unit: A legal dwelling unit in New York must have its own kitchen, bathroom, and living space. Basement conversions without proper kitchen facilities are not legal apartments.
  • Egress compliance: Each unit must have proper egress windows and a second means of exit in case of fire. I check for this during every showing.

Many two-family homes in Richmond Hill and South Ozone Park have been modified over the decades — rooms added, basements finished, attics converted. I know how to identify which modifications were done with permits and which ones will create problems at closing or during an appraisal.

FHA Loans for First-Generation Guyanese Buyers

FHA loans are the most popular financing option for first-time Guyanese homebuyers in Queens. Here's why they work so well for this community:

  • 3.5% down payment: On a $700,000 two-family home in Richmond Hill, the minimum down payment is $24,500 — far less than the 20% ($140,000) required by conventional loans without PMI.
  • Gift funds allowed: Your parents, siblings, or other family members can gift you the entire down payment. The lender requires a gift letter stating the money does not need to be repaid.
  • Rental income counts: For two-family properties, FHA allows you to count 75% of the projected rental income from the second unit toward your qualifying income. This is critical for Guyanese families buying two-family homes.
  • Lower credit score requirements: FHA accepts credit scores as low as 580 for the 3.5% down option. Many first-generation immigrants have limited credit history rather than bad credit — I work with lenders who understand the difference.
  • Available to legal residents: Green card holders, TPS holders, and authorized workers with valid Social Security numbers all qualify.

SONYMA for Additional Help

The State of New York Mortgage Agency offers first-time homebuyer programs that can be combined with FHA financing. Benefits include below-market interest rates and up to $15,000 in down payment assistance. Income limits apply, but many Guyanese families in Queens qualify. I walk my clients through the SONYMA application and the required homebuyer education course.

Cultural Considerations I Understand

Working with Guyanese families is not the same as working with any other buyer. I've learned this through years of experience. Here's what I bring to the table that a generic agent does not:

  • Family decision-making: Buying a home is a family decision. I expect and welcome multiple family members at showings, meetings, and closings. I don't rush the process.
  • Community connectivity: Proximity to the temple, the masjid, the church, the cultural center — these matter. I know the map.
  • Self-employment documentation: Many Guyanese homebuyers are self-employed — restaurant owners, taxi operators, construction workers, beauty salon owners. I connect them with lenders who offer bank statement programs designed for self-employed borrowers.
  • Remittance considerations: Regular wire transfers to Guyana can raise questions during the mortgage underwriting process. I help clients document these transfers properly so they don't delay the loan.
  • Investment mindset: Guyanese families think of homeownership as an investment, not just shelter. I focus on properties with rental income potential, appreciation trajectories, and the option to build equity that benefits the next generation.

Community Resources for Guyanese Homebuyers

Beyond my services, here are resources available to Guyanese homebuyers in Queens:

  • Homebuyer education courses: Required for SONYMA and some FHA programs. Several are offered in Queens with evening and weekend schedules.
  • NYCHA Section 8 homeownership program: Voucher holders may be eligible to use their voucher toward mortgage payments instead of rent.
  • NYC HomeFirst down payment assistance: Up to $100,000 for income-qualified buyers purchasing in New York City.
  • Legal aid for immigration-related questions: Organizations in Richmond Hill provide free legal consultations for buyers with immigration concerns.

FAQ — Guyanese Homebuyers in Queens

What are the best Queens neighborhoods for Guyanese homebuyers?

Richmond Hill is the cultural center of the Guyanese community, followed by South Ozone Park, Ozone Park, Howard Beach, and Jamaica. These neighborhoods offer Caribbean grocery stores, roti shops, cultural organizations, and established community networks. I specialize in all of these areas.

Can first-generation Guyanese immigrants qualify for FHA loans?

Yes. FHA loans are available to all legal US residents including green card holders and authorized workers. The minimum down payment is 3.5% and the credit score requirement is 580 or above. Gift funds from family are allowed. I connect clients with lenders who understand immigrant financial profiles.

How do I find a multi-generational home in Queens?

Look for legal two-family or three-family homes with separate entrances, kitchens, and bathrooms. I search specifically for properties with proper certificate of occupancy documentation and separate utility meters. Richmond Hill and South Ozone Park have the largest inventory of these properties.

What closing costs should Guyanese homebuyers expect?

Closing costs in Queens run 3% to 5% of the purchase price. For a $600,000 two-family home, expect $18,000 to $30,000 including mortgage origination, title insurance, attorney fees, and prepaid taxes. I provide a detailed estimate early so there are no surprises.
