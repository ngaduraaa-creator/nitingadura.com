Queens Market Conditions: What the Numbers Tell Us

The Queens real estate market in 2026 is defined by two competing forces: strong buyer demand supported by a gradually improving mortgage rate environment, and persistently low inventory as homeowners with sub-4% locked-in rates remain reluctant to sell. The result is a market that favors sellers but punishes overpricing more severely than it did in 2021 or 2022.

Mortgage rates in the mid-6% range are keeping monthly payments elevated, which means buyer budgets are tighter than they were during the sub-3% rate era. However, Queens remains remarkably affordable compared to Manhattan and brownstone Brooklyn, which continues to drive demand from buyers who want to own in New York City without spending $1.5 million on a modest home.

The most important trend I'm seeing across my transactions: pricing accuracy is everything. Homes listed at or within 2-3% of true market value are selling within 2-3 weeks, often with multiple offers. Homes priced 5% or more above market are sitting for 60-90 days and ultimately selling below what they would have achieved with accurate initial pricing.

What's Driving the Queens Market in 2026

Demand Drivers

  • Affordability relative to Manhattan and Brooklyn. A single-family home in Ozone Park costs what a one-bedroom condo costs in Park Slope. Queens remains the best value in NYC for homeowners who want space, a yard, and actual ownership.
  • Immigration-driven demand. Queens is the most ethnically diverse county in the United States, and immigrant families continue to drive strong demand for multi-family homes that can house extended family or generate rental income.
  • Remote work persistence. Hybrid and remote work arrangements continue to keep demand strong in neighborhoods with good transit access but more space than Manhattan — Astoria, Long Island City, Jackson Heights, and Sunnyside all benefit.
  • Investment appeal. Two and three-family homes in Southeast Queens offer cap rates that institutional investors can only dream of. Owner-occupant investors using FHA loans are acquiring cash-flowing properties with as little as 3.5% down.

Supply Constraints

  • Rate lock-in effect. Homeowners who locked in 2.5-3.5% mortgage rates during 2020-2022 are extremely reluctant to sell and take on a new mortgage at 6.5%. This is suppressing inventory across every neighborhood.
  • Limited new construction. Queens' zoning limits and construction costs mean very few new single-family or small multi-family homes are being built. New development is concentrated in large-scale condo and rental projects in Long Island City, Flushing, and Jamaica.
  • Aging homeowner population. Many Queens homeowners are aging in place rather than selling and downsizing. When these properties eventually come to market — often through estate sales — they will represent significant inventory increases in neighborhoods like Ozone Park, Howard Beach, and Bayside.

My Forecast for the Rest of 2026

Based on the data I'm tracking and the transactions I'm closing, here's where I see the Queens market heading for the remainder of 2026:

  • Prices: I expect 4-6% year-over-year appreciation to continue through Q3 2026, with potentially softening in Q4 as seasonal patterns take hold. Neighborhoods with very low inventory (Ridgewood, Maspeth, Astoria, Elmhurst) may see higher appreciation.
  • Inventory: Will tick up slightly as some homeowners who have been waiting to sell finally accept that sub-4% rates aren't coming back. But I don't expect a meaningful inventory surge.
  • Days on market: Will remain stable at 30-45 days for properly priced homes. Overpriced listings will continue to sit for 60-90+ days.
  • Mortgage rates: If rates decline to the 5.5-6% range as some economists predict, buyer demand will accelerate and push prices higher, particularly for entry-level homes in the $500K-$700K range.

Common Questions

FAQ — Queens Real Estate Market 2026

What is the median home price in Queens in 2026?

The Queens-wide median is approximately $685,000 for single-family homes and $775,000 for two-family properties. Prices vary enormously by neighborhood, from around $480,000 in parts of Far Rockaway and Jamaica to over $1,050,000 in Forest Hills.

Which Queens neighborhoods have the fastest-rising prices?

Ridgewood (up approximately 8.2%), Maspeth (7.5%), and South Ozone Park (6.8%) are showing the strongest year-over-year appreciation, driven by relative affordability and strong rental demand.

How long does it take to sell a house in Queens in 2026?

The average is 38 days across Queens, but properly priced properties in high-demand neighborhoods like Astoria and Long Island City can sell in under 20 days. Overpriced properties in any neighborhood can sit for 90+ days.

Is 2026 a good time to sell a house in Queens?

Yes, for most homeowners. Inventory remains below pre-pandemic levels and median prices continue to set records. However, pricing accuracy is critical — overpriced listings are sitting longer than during the 2021-2022 frenzy.

What are the best Queens neighborhoods for investment in 2026?

South Ozone Park, Jamaica, Cambria Heights, and Far Rockaway offer the strongest cap rates (5-7%). Ridgewood, Maspeth, and Woodhaven balance appreciation potential with solid rental returns.
