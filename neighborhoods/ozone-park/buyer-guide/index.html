Buying a Home in Ozone Park — My Step-by-Step Buyer's Guide

By Nitin Gadura · Lic. #10401383405 · Updated 2026-04-20

I've guided dozens of families through the home-buying process in Ozone Park and neighboring Queens communities. Here's exactly what I tell every buyer client before we start our search.

The Ozone Park Market Reality

The A-train terminus at Ozone Park–Lefferts Blvd puts Midtown Manhattan just 40 minutes away. With median home prices below Forest Hills and Jackson Heights, this is one of Queens' best value propositions.

Median price: $755,000. Average days on market: 25 days. Translation: you need to be pre-approved and ready to act fast. I've seen buyers lose homes they loved because they weren't prepared.

Step 1 — Get Pre-Approved (Before Anything Else)

In today's Ozone Park market, a pre-approval letter is non-negotiable. Sellers won't entertain an offer without one. I work with local lenders who know Queens co-op and multi-family financing — these nuances matter enormously in this market.

Tip from me: Get pre-approved by a Queens-based lender AND a national lender. Compare rates. Even 0.25% difference on a $755,000 loan is ~$45,300 over 30 years.

Step 2 — Define Your Must-Haves vs Nice-to-Haves

In Ozone Park, the housing stock is primarily 2-family homes (50% of listings). Be clear before we look: are you open to a 2-family where you rent one unit? That option dramatically expands your buying power.

Step 3 — Making an Offer That Wins

Here's what works in Ozone Park right now:

  • Come in clean: Fewer contingencies = more attractive to sellers
  • Escalation clause: I use these strategically — they win without overpaying
  • Strong earnest money: 2–3% shows you're serious
  • Flexible closing: Ask the seller what date works for them — this gesture alone has won me multiple deals

Step 4 — Inspections in Ozone Park

Never waive the inspection without doing a pre-inspection walk-through first. Older Queens homes (many built 1920s–1960s) often have: aging boilers, updated-but-not-permitted electrical, basement moisture, or original cast iron plumbing. A good inspector catches these. I know excellent Queens home inspectors at every price point.

Step 5 — Closing Costs to Budget

  • Mortgage recording tax: 1.8–1.925% of loan amount
  • Title insurance: ~$1,500–$3,000
  • Attorney: $1,200–$2,500 (required in NY — I can refer you to experienced real estate attorneys)
  • Mansion Tax: 1% on purchases over $1M (buyer pays)
  • Total buyer costs: typically 2–4% of purchase price

First-Time Buyer? You May Get $100,000

NYC HPD's HomeFirst program provides up to $100,000 in down payment assistance for income-eligible first-time buyers. I've helped several clients access this program — it's real, it works, and most people don't know about it.
