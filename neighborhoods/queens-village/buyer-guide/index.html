Buying a Home in Queens Village — My Step-by-Step Buyer's Guide

By Nitin Gadura · Lic. #10401383405 · Updated 2026-04-20

I've guided dozens of families through the home-buying process in Queens Village and neighboring Queens communities. Here's exactly what I tell every buyer client before we start our search.

The Queens Village Market Reality

Queens Village's Nassau County border location offers the best of both worlds: suburban Colonial and Cape Cod homes, LIRR commuter access, and NYC property taxes rather than Nassau's.

Median price: $722,000. Average days on market: 30 days. Translation: you need to be pre-approved and ready to act fast. I've seen buyers lose homes they loved because they weren't prepared.

Step 1 — Get Pre-Approved (Before Anything Else)

In today's Queens Village market, a pre-approval letter is non-negotiable. Sellers won't entertain an offer without one. I work with local lenders who know Queens co-op and multi-family financing — these nuances matter enormously in this market.

Tip from me: Get pre-approved by a Queens-based lender AND a national lender. Compare rates. Even 0.25% difference on a $722,000 loan is ~$43,320 over 30 years.

Step 2 — Define Your Must-Haves vs Nice-to-Haves

In Queens Village, the housing stock is primarily 2-family homes (40% of listings). Be clear before we look: are you open to a 2-family where you rent one unit? That option dramatically expands your buying power.

Step 3 — Making an Offer That Wins

Here's what works in Queens Village right now:

  • Come in clean: Fewer contingencies = more attractive to sellers
  • Escalation clause: I use these strategically — they win without overpaying
  • Strong earnest money: 2–3% shows you're serious
  • Flexible closing: Ask the seller what date works for them — this gesture alone has won me multiple deals

Step 4 — Inspections in Queens Village

Never waive the inspection without doing a pre-inspection walk-through first. Older Queens homes (many built 1920s–1960s) often have: aging boilers, updated-but-not-permitted electrical, basement moisture, or original cast iron plumbing. A good inspector catches these. I know excellent Queens home inspectors at every price point.

Step 5 — Closing Costs to Budget

  • Mortgage recording tax: 1.8–1.925% of loan amount
  • Title insurance: ~$1,500–$3,000
  • Attorney: $1,200–$2,500 (required in NY — I can refer you to experienced real estate attorneys)
  • Mansion Tax: 1% on purchases over $1M (buyer pays)
  • Total buyer costs: typically 2–4% of purchase price

First-Time Buyer? You May Get $100,000

NYC HPD's HomeFirst program provides up to $100,000 in down payment assistance for income-eligible first-time buyers. I've helped several clients access this program — it's real, it works, and most people don't know about it.
