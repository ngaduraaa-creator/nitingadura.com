Queens Real Estate Glossary

50+ terms explained in plain English — including New York-specific vocabulary that buyers, sellers, and co-op shareholders need to know.

ABCD EFHI LMNO PRST UZ
A
Agency Disclosure NY Law
A written form that New York real estate agents must provide — and have signed — at the first substantive contact with a prospective buyer or seller. It explains whether the agent represents the seller, the buyer, or both parties (dual agency). Required under NY RPL §443. Failure to provide this disclosure is a license violation.
Amortization
The process of paying off a loan through regular installment payments over time. In a fixed-rate mortgage, each payment reduces the principal balance slightly more than the previous one — so early payments are mostly interest, while later payments are mostly principal. A 30-year mortgage is fully amortized over 360 monthly payments.
Appraisal
An independent professional opinion of a property's market value, conducted by a licensed appraiser. Mortgage lenders require appraisals to confirm that the property is worth at least the loan amount. If the appraisal comes in below the contract price, the buyer may need to renegotiate, pay the difference in cash, or cancel the contract (if they have an appraisal contingency).
Attorney Review Period NY Practice
In New York, contracts of sale are typically prepared by the seller's attorney and reviewed by the buyer's attorney before the buyer signs. There is no set statutory "attorney review period" as in New Jersey — instead, in New York it is standard practice for both sides to negotiate contract terms through their attorneys before any binding agreement is signed. The buyer is not legally bound until both sides have signed the contract.
B
Board Package Co-op
A comprehensive application submitted by a co-op buyer to the co-op board for approval. Typically includes financial statements, tax returns, bank statements, employment verification, personal references, and a letter of intent. The board has broad discretion to approve or reject buyers, as long as the rejection does not violate Fair Housing laws. Board packages often take 4–8 weeks to process in Queens co-ops.
Bridge Loan
A short-term loan used to "bridge" the gap between selling one home and buying another. A homeowner who needs the equity from their current home to purchase a new one might use a bridge loan to fund the down payment before their old home closes. High-interest and short-term — typically 6–12 months.
C
C of O / Certificate of Occupancy NY Law
A document issued by the NYC Department of Buildings confirming that a structure was built or renovated in compliance with the NYC Building Code and is safe for occupancy. Missing or incorrect Certificates of Occupancy — particularly for finished basements, attic conversions, or added rooms built without permits — are among the most common closing issues in Queens. Buyers and lenders require an accurate C of O. Governed by NYC Administrative Code §28-118.
Closing Disclosure (CD)
A federally mandated form (required by TRID/RESPA) that lenders must provide to buyers at least 3 business days before closing. It itemizes all final loan terms, monthly payments, and closing costs. Buyers should compare it carefully to their Loan Estimate to identify any unexpected changes.
CMA — Comparative Market Analysis
A professional pricing analysis prepared by a real estate agent using recently sold comparable properties ("comps") to estimate the current market value of a home. A CMA is not an appraisal — it is an agent's data-backed opinion of value used to guide listing price strategy or buyer offer decisions. I provide free CMAs for Queens homes.
Contingency
A condition written into a contract of sale that must be satisfied for the transaction to proceed. Common contingencies in Queens include: mortgage contingency (buyer must obtain financing), inspection contingency, and appraisal contingency. If a contingency is not met and is properly exercised, the buyer can typically cancel the contract and recover their deposit.
D
DOM — Days on Market
The number of days a property has been actively listed for sale in the MLS. High DOM can signal overpricing or issues with the property. In competitive Queens neighborhoods, well-priced homes often go into contract within 7–14 days. When a listing is relisted after being taken off the market, it may reset the DOM counter — which can obscure how long a property has actually been available.
Down Payment
The portion of the purchase price paid by the buyer in cash, separate from the mortgage. In New York, buyers typically put 10–20% down on 1–4 family homes and may be required to put 20–25% down on co-ops. Programs like NYC HomeFirst and SONYMA can provide funds toward the down payment for qualifying first-time buyers. Note: New York does not use "earnest money" the way other states do — see "Down Payment (NY Contract)" below.
Down Payment — NY Contract Practice NY Practice
Unlike most states, New York does not use a separate "earnest money" deposit held in escrow before contract signing. Instead, buyers typically pay a 10% deposit at the time of signing the contract of sale, which is held in escrow by the seller's attorney. This deposit can be at risk if the buyer defaults without a valid contractual reason.
Dual Agency
When a single licensed broker represents both the buyer and the seller in the same transaction. Dual agency is legal in New York with written informed consent from both parties (disclosed via the Agency Disclosure Form). However, it limits the agent's ability to negotiate aggressively on either side's behalf. In a dual agency, the agent cannot disclose the seller's minimum acceptable price to the buyer or vice versa.
E
Easement
A legal right for one party to use a portion of another party's property for a specific purpose — such as a utility company's right to access underground pipes or a neighbor's right to use a shared driveway. Easements are disclosed in the title search and run with the land, meaning they transfer to new owners at closing.
Escrow
A neutral holding arrangement where funds or documents are held by a third party until certain conditions are met. In New York, the buyer's 10% contract deposit is held in escrow by the seller's attorney (or a neutral third party) until closing. The seller's attorney is not permitted to release these funds before closing without the buyer's consent, except as provided by the contract.
F
Fair Housing Act
Federal law (42 U.S.C. §3604) prohibiting discrimination in housing based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, or disability. New York State and NYC add additional protected classes including source of income, sexual orientation, gender identity, immigration status, and lawful occupation. Violations can result in significant penalties. All real estate advertising and representation must comply.
Flip Tax Co-op
A transfer fee charged by a co-op corporation when a shareholder sells their apartment. The flip tax is typically paid by the seller and calculated as a percentage of the sale price, a fixed amount per share, or a percentage of the profit. It can range from 1–3% of the sale price in Queens co-ops. The existence and amount of a flip tax must be disclosed to buyers and is found in the co-op's proprietary lease or house rules.
First Right of Refusal
A contractual right that gives a specific party (often a co-op corporation, existing tenant, or partner) the first opportunity to purchase a property before the seller accepts an offer from an outside buyer. Many NYC condominiums have a right of first refusal for the condo board, though it is rarely exercised.
FSBO — For Sale By Owner
A property listed directly by the owner without the representation of a listing agent. FSBO sellers avoid paying a listing agent's commission but take on the full responsibility of pricing, marketing, negotiating, and managing the transaction. In NYC's complex legal environment, most FSBO transactions still require real estate attorneys on both sides.
H
HOA — Homeowners Association
An organization governing a community of homes, condos, or townhouses. HOA fees pay for shared amenities, exterior maintenance, landscaping, and reserves. HOAs can impose rules on exterior appearance, rentals, and renovations. Queens townhouse and condo communities may have HOAs — check monthly fees and any special assessments before purchasing.
HUD — U.S. Dept. of Housing and Urban Development
The federal agency overseeing housing policy, FHA mortgage insurance, Fair Housing enforcement, and approved homebuyer counseling agencies. HUD-approved housing counseling is free or low-cost and satisfies the education requirement for many first-time buyer programs in Queens.
I
Inspection Contingency
A contract clause allowing the buyer to have the property professionally inspected within a specified timeframe. If the inspection reveals significant defects, the buyer may request repairs, a price reduction, or cancel the contract. In NYC competitive markets, buyers sometimes waive inspection contingencies — but this carries real risk, especially in Queens where older homes may have hidden issues like knob-and-tube wiring, asbestos, or deferred maintenance.
L
Letter of Testamentary NY Probate
A document issued by the Surrogate's Court authorizing an executor to act on behalf of a deceased person's estate. When a Queens property owner dies and leaves real estate, the executor must obtain Letters Testamentary before they can legally sell the property. This process can take months — and affects the transaction timeline in estate sales.
Lis Pendens NY Law
Latin for "pending lawsuit." A Lis Pendens is a public notice filed in county court records indicating that litigation involving a property is pending — typically in foreclosure proceedings or ownership disputes. A property with an active Lis Pendens cannot be transferred cleanly until the underlying lawsuit is resolved. A thorough title search will reveal any Lis Pendens on record at the Queens County Clerk's office.
Loan-to-Value Ratio (LTV)
The ratio of the mortgage loan amount to the appraised value of the property, expressed as a percentage. A $400,000 loan on a $500,000 property = 80% LTV. Lenders use LTV to assess risk — higher LTV loans are riskier and may require private mortgage insurance (PMI). Most conventional lenders prefer LTV at or below 80%.
M
Mansion Tax NY Tax
A New York State and NYC transfer tax paid by the buyer on residential purchases of $1 million or more. The state rate starts at 1% on purchases of $1M–$1,999,999 and rises incrementally up to 3.9% for purchases of $25M or more. The NYC mansion tax applies separately for purchases within NYC. The mansion tax is paid at closing and is typically the buyer's obligation.
MLS — Multiple Listing Service
A cooperative database of property listings shared among real estate brokers. In New York City and Queens, the dominant MLS is operated by the Real Estate Board of New York (REBNY) and OneKey MLS. When a property is listed on the MLS, it becomes visible to thousands of buyer's agents and platforms like Zillow, StreetEasy, and Realtor.com. FSBO sellers can pay for MLS access through flat-fee listing services.
Mortgage Contingency
A contract clause that makes the purchase contingent on the buyer obtaining a mortgage commitment at a specified loan amount and interest rate. If the buyer cannot secure financing, they can cancel the contract and recover their deposit. Most Queens contracts of sale include a mortgage contingency — its absence puts the buyer's deposit at risk if they cannot get a loan.
N
NOD — Notice of Default
A formal notice filed by a lender when a borrower is significantly delinquent on mortgage payments. In New York, which is a judicial foreclosure state, the NOD is part of the pre-foreclosure process. After the NOD, the lender can proceed to file a foreclosure lawsuit. Homeowners who receive an NOD have legal options including loan modification, short sale, or deed in lieu of foreclosure.
O
Offering Plan NY / Condo
A New York State Attorney General-approved document that describes a condominium or cooperative conversion or new development. The offering plan (sometimes called the "Red Herring" in draft form) details the building's physical condition, financials, management, and the rights and obligations of owners. Buyers of condos and co-ops are entitled to review the offering plan and must receive a copy before signing a contract.
P
PCDS — Property Condition Disclosure Statement NY Law
A New York State-mandated disclosure form (NY RPL §462) in which the seller answers specific questions about the known condition of the property — including roof, foundation, plumbing, electrical, heating, environmental issues, and more. If the seller refuses to provide the PCDS, they must give the buyer a $500 credit at closing. Completing the PCDS fully and accurately protects sellers from post-closing claims of nondisclosure.
PMI — Private Mortgage Insurance
Insurance required by most conventional lenders when the buyer's down payment is less than 20% (LTV greater than 80%). PMI protects the lender — not the buyer — if the borrower defaults. On a conventional loan, PMI can be removed once the homeowner builds 20% equity. FHA loans have their own version called MIP (Mortgage Insurance Premium), which is typically permanent for the life of the loan unless refinanced.
Proprietary Lease Co-op
The legal document that defines a co-op shareholder's right to occupy their specific apartment unit. In a co-op, you do not own real property — you own shares in the co-op corporation and hold a proprietary lease to your unit. The proprietary lease sets out the shareholder's rights and obligations, including maintenance fees, subletting rules, renovation approval requirements, and grounds for eviction by the co-op corporation.
R
Radon Disclosure
Radon is a naturally occurring radioactive gas that can accumulate in basements and lower levels of homes. New York requires disclosure of any known radon issues in the Property Condition Disclosure Statement. Buyers should consider radon testing as part of their home inspection — remediation systems are available and effective if elevated levels are found.
RPL §443 — Real Property Law Section 443 NY Law
The New York State statute requiring real estate licensees to disclose their agency relationship (seller's agent, buyer's agent, or dual agent) in writing at the first substantive contact with a prospective buyer or seller. The signed Agency Disclosure Form created under this statute must be kept in the broker's transaction file. See also: Agency Disclosure.
RP-5217 NY Form
New York State's Real Property Transfer Report — a mandatory form filed with the deed at the county clerk's office at the time of every property sale. It collects data on the sale price, type of property, financing, and other transaction characteristics for assessment and statistical purposes. Without the RP-5217, the deed cannot be recorded. In NYC the form is RP-5217-NYC. See also: TP-584.
S
Short Sale
A sale in which the lender agrees to accept less than the full mortgage balance owed. Short sales occur when a homeowner is underwater (owes more than the home is worth) and cannot continue making payments. The lender must approve the short sale price. In New York, the process can take 3–6 months and involves significant lender paperwork. The seller may face tax consequences on the forgiven debt (consult a tax advisor).
SONYMA — State of New York Mortgage Agency NY Program
A New York State public benefit corporation that offers below-market fixed-rate mortgage programs for first-time homebuyers. SONYMA's primary programs — Achieving the Dream and Low Interest Rate — are available in Queens through participating lenders and can be combined with down payment assistance programs like NYC HomeFirst. See the First-Time Homebuyer Programs page for details.
Surrogate's Court NY Probate
The New York State court with jurisdiction over probate proceedings, estates, and guardianships. When a Queens homeowner dies, the Surrogate's Court for Queens County oversees the administration of their estate — including the issuance of Letters Testamentary or Letters of Administration that authorize an executor or administrator to sell estate real property. Surrogate's Court for Queens is located in Jamaica.
T
Title Insurance NY Practice
Insurance protecting against defects in the chain of title — including undisclosed liens, forgery, fraud, errors in public records, or prior unrecorded claims. In New York, both the buyer's lender (lender's policy) and the buyer (owner's policy) typically purchase title insurance. Uniquely in New York, the buyer generally pays for title insurance — unlike most other states where the seller pays. The cost is a one-time premium at closing.
TP-584 NY Form
New York State's Combined Real Estate Transfer Tax Return — a required form filed by the seller (or seller's attorney) at or before closing documenting the property sale and calculating the applicable state transfer tax. In NYC transactions, the NYC RPTT (Real Property Transfer Tax) is also reported at closing. Both TP-584 and RP-5217 must be filed together with the deed at the county clerk's office for recording to occur.
Transfer Tax NY Tax
A tax imposed by New York State and New York City on the transfer of real property. The NY State transfer tax is $2 per $500 of consideration (0.4%) for most residential sales, with an additional 1% on sales of $1M or more. NYC imposes an additional Real Property Transfer Tax (RPTT) of 1%–2.625% depending on the property type and price. Transfer taxes are generally paid by the seller, except for new construction condos where the buyer typically pays. All transfer taxes are filed at closing.
U
Underlying Mortgage Co-op
The master mortgage held by the co-op corporation on the entire building — as distinct from any individual shareholder's personal financing. When a co-op has a large underlying mortgage with high monthly payments per unit, it can significantly increase the shareholder's monthly maintenance charges. Buyers should review the co-op's financials carefully to understand the underlying mortgage balance, interest rate, and maturity date before purchasing shares.
Z
Zoning
Municipal regulations that determine how land can be used — residential, commercial, industrial, mixed-use — and what can be built on it. In Queens, the NYC Department of City Planning administers zoning through the NYC Zoning Resolution. Common Queens residential zones include R2 (single-family), R3 through R6 (low to medium density residential). Buyers considering renovations, additions, or change of use should verify zoning compliance through the NYC Zoning Map and consult with an architect before closing.
