NY Home Seller Legal Checklist

Every required disclosure, document, and legal step for selling a home in Queens, New York — with citations to NY state law.

How to use this checklist: New York state law imposes specific disclosure and documentation requirements on sellers. Missing or late disclosures can delay closing, expose you to liability, or allow buyers to cancel the contract. Work through each item with your attorney and agent well before your target closing date. Click items to mark them complete.

Required Disclosures

Agency Disclosure Form

New York law requires that your agent provide and have you sign an Agency Disclosure form explaining the agent's representation relationship — whether they represent the seller, the buyer, or both (dual agency). This must be provided at the first substantive contact with a prospective buyer.

NY RPL §443

Property Condition Disclosure Statement (PCDS)

Sellers must complete and deliver a PCDS disclosing the known condition of the property — including roof, foundation, plumbing, electrical, environmental issues, and more. If you choose not to provide it, you must give the buyer a $500 credit at closing. Most attorneys recommend completing it fully.

NY RPL §462

Lead Paint Disclosure

If your home was built before 1978, federal law requires you to disclose any known lead-based paint hazards to the buyer. You must provide the EPA-approved pamphlet "Protect Your Family from Lead in Your Home" and include a lead paint disclosure addendum in the contract. Buyers are entitled to a 10-day inspection period for lead paint.

42 U.S.C. §4852d — HUD/EPA Rule

Oil Tank Disclosure

If the property has or had an underground oil storage tank (UST), you must disclose its existence, condition, and any prior environmental testing. Undisclosed tanks can expose sellers to environmental liability long after closing. A Phase I or Phase II environmental assessment may be requested by buyers.

6 NYCRR Part 613 (NYS DEC)

Smoke Detector & Carbon Monoxide Detector Certification

New York law requires working smoke detectors on every floor and within 15 feet of all sleeping areas. Carbon monoxide detectors are required within 15 feet of sleeping areas in homes with fossil fuel appliances or attached garages. Sellers typically provide a certification of compliance at closing.

NY Executive Law §378 — NY Public Health Law §1228

Title & Ownership Documents

Original Deed

Locate your original deed or request a certified copy from the Queens County Clerk's Office. Your attorney will prepare a new deed to transfer title to the buyer at closing. The property description in the deed must match exactly.

Certificate of Occupancy (C of O)

A Certificate of Occupancy confirms that the home was built and/or modified with proper permits and complies with the NYC Building Code. Missing or incorrect C of Os — especially for finished basements, additions, or converted spaces — are among the most common closing delays in Queens. Verify your C of O matches the current structure.

NYC Administrative Code §28-118

Title Insurance Policy (Owner's)

Locate your existing owner's title insurance policy. While the buyer will purchase their own title insurance, your existing policy may contain information relevant to the transaction, including prior surveys, easements, and deed restrictions that must be disclosed.

Survey

An updated survey showing property lines, structures, easements, and encroachments is typically required by the buyer's lender and title company. If you have an existing survey, your attorney can determine whether it is acceptable or whether a new survey is needed.

Financial & Tax Forms

Mortgage Payoff Statement

Order a payoff statement from your lender at least 2 weeks before closing. The payoff amount includes your remaining principal plus accrued interest and any prepayment penalties. Your attorney will use this to ensure the mortgage is properly discharged at closing.

TP-584 — Combined Real Estate Transfer Tax Return

Required by New York State for all conveyances of real property. The seller completes this form and the applicable transfer tax is paid at or before closing. In NYC, sellers also pay the NYC Real Property Transfer Tax (RPTT) on the same transaction.

NY Tax Law §1409 — Form TP-584

RP-5217 — Real Property Transfer Report

This New York State form collects transaction data for property assessment and statistical purposes and must be filed with the deed at the county clerk's office. Your attorney typically prepares this. Without it, the deed cannot be recorded.

NY RPTL §333 — Form RP-5217-NYC

NYC Real Property Transfer Tax (RPTT)

NYC imposes a transfer tax on the seller for residential sales — 1% for properties under $500K and 1.425% for $500K and above (plus state transfer tax). For sales of $3M or more, an additional Mansion Tax (paid by the buyer) also applies. Confirm exact rates with your attorney.

NYC Admin Code §11-2102

Professional Retention

Real Estate Attorney Retained

Unlike many states, New York real estate practice strongly favors — and in most transactions effectively requires — that sellers retain their own licensed real estate attorney. Your attorney will draft or review the contract, negotiate terms, handle all title and transfer issues, and attend closing. Retain your attorney before accepting any offer.

Real Estate Agent / Listing Agreement Signed

A written listing agreement between you and your broker sets the listing price, commission rate, listing term, and marketing plan. In New York, listing agreements must be in writing and signed by both parties to be enforceable. This agreement is typically an Exclusive Right to Sell.

NY RPL §443-a

HOA, Co-op & Condo Documents (if applicable)

HOA Documents & Financials

If the property is part of a Homeowners Association, provide the buyer with the governing documents (bylaws, CC&Rs, rules), the most recent budget and reserve fund study, and any pending special assessments or litigation. Buyers typically have a review period after receiving these.

Co-op Board Package & Board Approval (Co-op only)

Co-op sales require buyer approval by the co-op board. The seller must provide the proprietary lease, house rules, and most recent financial statements. The buyer must complete a board package — which can take 4–8 weeks. Many co-ops also charge a flip tax to the seller at closing.

Condo Offering Plan & Financials (Condo only)

Condo sales require delivery of the building's current offering plan, most recent three years of financial statements, house rules, and any pending special assessments. The condo board may have a right of first refusal but typically does not have the same blocking power as a co-op board.

Pre-Closing & Closing Day

Final Walkthrough by Buyer

Buyers are entitled to a final walkthrough — typically 24–48 hours before closing — to confirm the property is in the agreed condition, all agreed repairs have been made, and no items have been removed that were included in the sale. The property must be broom clean with all personal property removed unless agreed otherwise.

Utility Transfers & Final Readings

Arrange final meter readings for gas, electric, and water. Transfer or cancel utility accounts effective on the closing date. In NYC, open water/sewer charges become a lien on the property if unpaid — confirm all charges are current with the NYC DEP before closing.

Keys, Remotes & Access Items

Gather all keys to the property (front door, back door, garage, mailbox, storage), garage door remotes, gate codes, alarm codes, and any parking permits or building FOBs. These are turned over at the closing table.

Valid Government-Issued Photo ID

All sellers listed on the deed must appear at closing with a current, valid government-issued photo ID (driver's license, passport). If a seller cannot attend, a Power of Attorney may be arranged — but must be prepared well in advance and approved by all parties.
