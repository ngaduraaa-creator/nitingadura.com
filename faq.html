Selling a Home in Queens

Well-priced homes typically go into contract in 30–60 days. From accepted offer to closing is another 45–75 days. Total time from listing to closing is usually 75–135 days, depending on market conditions, buyer financing, and attorney timelines.

NY Real Estate Laws & Process

New York RPL §443 requires agents to present and discuss an Agency Disclosure form at the first substantive meeting with a buyer or seller. The form explains who the agent represents — seller's agent, buyer's agent, or dual agent — so you know whose interests are being served. Signing the form is an acknowledgment, not a commitment to work with that agent.
The 2024 NAR settlement changed buyer agent compensation rules. Sellers are no longer required to offer buyer agent compensation in MLS listings. Buyers must sign a written Buyer Representation Agreement before touring with an agent, making agent compensation more transparent upfront. The amount is negotiable between buyer and agent.
A CO issued by the NYC Dept. of Buildings confirms a structure is safe and legal for its stated use. Before buying any Queens property — especially one with a converted basement or added structure — verify the CO matches the actual layout. Illegal conversions can complicate financing, insurance, and legal occupancy.
A 1031 exchange (IRS Section 1031) lets investors defer capital gains taxes by reinvesting proceeds from a sold investment property into a like-kind property. Strict timelines apply: 45 days to identify a replacement, 180 days to close. Must be handled through a qualified intermediary. Consult a CPA or tax attorney.
Budget 3%–5% of the purchase price. This covers: mortgage origination, title insurance, attorney fees ($1,500–$2,500), NYC mansion tax (if $1M+), recording fees, and prepaid escrows. Your lender is required to provide a Loan Estimate within 3 business days of application — review it carefully.
NY's homestead exemption (CPLR §5206) protects up to $179,975 of your primary residence's equity from general creditors in NYC, Nassau, and Suffolk counties. It's automatic — no annual application required. Note: this is separate from the STAR tax exemption, which does require registration.

Co-ops & Condos

Condo: you own the unit as real property with a deed. Flexible subletting, no board approval to purchase. Typically more expensive. Co-op: you own shares in a corporation. Requires board approval to purchase, stricter financial rules, possible flip taxes and subletting limits. Co-ops dominate Queens inventory and are often priced more accessibly than condos.
A flip tax is a transfer fee charged by some co-op buildings when a unit is sold, typically paid by the seller. It can range from 1%–3% of the sale price or be calculated per share. Not all co-ops have them — review the building's proprietary lease and house rules during due diligence.
After offer acceptance, the buyer submits a board package: financial statements, tax returns, bank statements, personal references, and employer letters. The board reviews and interviews the buyer, then votes. Process adds 4–8 weeks. Board rejections have no legal appeal. Nitin prepares buyers for board packages and helps select buildings with reasonable approval processes.
Monthly maintenance in a co-op covers your share of the building's operating costs including the underlying mortgage, real estate taxes, utilities for common areas, and building staff. Queens co-op maintenance fees typically range from $400–$1,200/month depending on the building, size, and location. A portion of maintenance is often tax-deductible — confirm with your accountant.
